3 Day Itinerary

When visiting Tokyo, you could stay for a lifetime and still not explore the entire city. However, even with just 3 days, you'll have ample time to see some of the greatest parts local Tokyo has to offer. This itinerary aims to highlight the must-visit spots while suggesting which overrated places you can skip.

Day 1

East Tokyo

Asakusa

Approximate Time for Sightseeing: 3-4 hours

Asakusa has been a prominent center of culture and entertainment since the Edo period (1603-1868). The area flourished as a temple town and later became a hub for theater and performing arts. Despite suffering damage during World War II, Asakusa has retained its historical charm and continues to be a cultural landmark.

When you reach the entrance to Asakusa, take a photo of the famous Kaminarimon red lantern, dedicated to the god of thunder Raijin, the protector of temple grounds from evil. As you walk down the narrow street called Nakamise-dori, do some window shopping and buy a snack if you are hungry. At the end of the path will be Sensoji Temple, founded in 628 AD to the Kannon Goddess. While there, you can try and inspect your fortune called Omikuji for a small fee. Afterwards, find a place to eat as there are many choices in the area.

Other Mentions

If you plan to visit various temples in Japan, consider getting a Goshūinchō (御朱印帳), which are unique stamps and calligraphy given by the temple or shrine as a record of the visit.Their main purpose to collect Goshūin, which serve as both a keepsake and a proof of visit to a particular religious site. They typically have a hardcover with beautiful, often traditional designs, and contains accordion-style folded pages to accommodate the stamps and calligraphy. They are also fun to collect!

Ueno and Tokyo Museum

Approximate Time for Sightseeing: 4-6 hours

After visiting Asakusa, head to Ueno. Ueno is a vibrant district in Tokyo known for its cultural attractions and beautiful park. The area is home to several museums, a zoo, and a variety of seasonal events. Ueno Park, established in 1873, is one of Japan's oldest public parks and a popular spot for cherry blossom viewing in the spring.

Start your visit at Ueno Park, where you can stroll through lush greenery and enjoy the serene atmosphere. The park hosts numerous events throughout the year, so be on the lookout for local festivals, art exhibitions, and yatai (food stalls) offering delicious Japanese street food. A must-see in Ueno is the Tokyo National Museum, the largest national museum in Japan, which houses an extensive collection of art and artifacts from Japan and other Asian countries.

Other notable attractions in Ueno include the Ueno Zoo, which is Japan's oldest zoo and home to a variety of animals, including the famous giant pandas. For art enthusiasts, the Tokyo Metropolitan Art Museum and the National Museum of Western Art are also located within the park.

Other Mentions

Watch for events that may occur frequently at Ueno Park. When they pop up, they generally have various street food stalls to try out various foods. Otherwise, visit Ameya-Yokocho market street nearby, where you can shop for souvenirs, try local delicacies, and experience the bustling market atmosphere.

Honorable Mentions

Tokyo Sky Tree

Approximate Time for Sightseeing: 2-3 hours

The Tokyo Skytree is the tallest structure in Japan, standing at 634 meters. It offers spectacular panoramic views of Tokyo from its observation decks, located at 350 meters and 450 meters. The Skytree also features a shopping complex, dining options, and an aquarium, making it a comprehensive entertainment destination. The surrounding Tokyo Solamachi area has numerous shops and restaurants to explore. However be wary as busy hours can easily double the waiting time.

Ryogoku

Approximate Time for Sightseeing: 2-3 hours

Ryogoku is best known as the heart of sumo wrestling in Tokyo. The area is home to the Ryogoku Kokugikan (Sumo Hall), where you can attend sumo tournaments held in January, May, and September. Even if there is no tournament, the Sumo Museum located inside the hall provides a fascinating look into the history and culture of sumo. Additionally, Ryogoku is home to the Edo-Tokyo Museum, which offers an immersive experience of Tokyo’s history from the Edo period to modern times. The area also has numerous traditional chanko nabe restaurants, serving the hearty stew eaten by sumo wrestlers. Recommended if you are a foodie.

Day 2

West Tokyo

Meiji Shrine

Approximate Time for Sightseeing: 1-2 hours

The **Meiji Shrine** is a serene Shinto shrine dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. Located in a lush forested area, it's a peaceful retreat from the urban environment. The shrine complex includes beautiful gardens, torii gates, and a treasure house. Visitors can also witness traditional Shinto ceremonies and rituals. The walk from the entrance through the forest to the shrine is a calming experience, making it a perfect spot for reflection and relaxation.

5 Day Itinerary

With 5 days, you give yourself more breathing space to visit the outskirts of Tokyo that outshine even the inner parts of the city. These trips generally take a full day, but there is enough time to depart from Tokyo and return if your lodging happens to be in Tokyo.

7+ Day Itinerary

There is no limit to the things you can do from Tokyo. If you give yourself 7 or more days, you now have the chance to venture even further to places that local Tokyoites would go. These trips would entail a overnight stay, so prepare to make accomodation elsewhere for a day or two.
